## Key Trading Strategies & ICT Concepts

The strategy discussed in the video is heavily based on **ICT (Inner Circle Trader)** concepts, which focus on price action and liquidity rather than traditional indicators. The following sections detail the key concepts and strategies explained, along with practical implementation guidance.

### 1. Multi-Timeframe Analysis

This strategy involves a hierarchical approach to market analysis, beginning with a **Higher Time Frame** (Daily) to establish an overarching market bias and then transitioning to **Lower Time Frames** (1-hour and 15-minute) for precise entry and confirmation. To implement this, traders should first identify the general market direction and significant price levels on a daily chart. Subsequently, they can narrow their focus to hourly and 15-minute charts to pinpoint specific entry signals that align with the broader daily trend, ensuring that short-term movements are contextualized within the long-term perspective.

### 2. Fair Value Gap (FVG)

A **Fair Value Gap (FVG)** represents an imbalance in price action, often appearing as a void or gap between candlesticks. These gaps indicate areas where buying or selling pressure was so intense that orders were not fully matched, creating an inefficiency in the market. Price frequently revisits these FVGs to 
fill these imbalances. The trader in the video emphasizes waiting for the price to "tap into" a daily FVG, anticipating a reaction or reversal from that level. For practical implementation, traders should identify FVGs on higher timeframes and, as price approaches these zones, observe lower timeframes for confirmation of a potential reversal or continuation, as these gaps often act as price magnets.

### 3. Balanced Price Range (BPR)

A **Balanced Price Range (BPR)**, as mentioned in the context of a "daily bearish BPR," signifies a specific price action where aggressive movements have occurred in both bullish and bearish directions, resulting in an overlapping price range. These BPRs can serve as robust resistance or support zones within the market. To utilize this concept, traders should identify BPRs on daily charts, recognizing them as powerful areas where price is likely to react. These zones can offer strategic entry or exit points, with a bearish BPR, for instance, indicating a strong resistance area.

### 4. Sell-Side Liquidity

**Sell-Side Liquidity** refers to concentrations of sell-stop orders, typically located below significant swing lows. Market participants often target these areas, as price tends to gravitate towards liquidity pools to facilitate order execution. In the video, the trader targets "internal lows" where liquidity is expected to reside. From an implementation standpoint, identifying these internal lows on charts can help define potential targets for short positions, as price frequently sweeps these levels before potentially reversing or continuing its trend.

### 5. FVG Inversion

**FVG Inversion** occurs when a Fair Value Gap, initially expected to function as support or resistance, is breached. Once broken, the FVG can "invert" its role, meaning a previously bullish FVG that was broken to the downside may subsequently act as a bearish resistance level upon retest. To implement this, traders should monitor FVGs that have been violated and observe if price retests the inverted FVG. This retest can present a high-probability entry point, aligning with the market's new directional bias.

### 6. Change in the State of Delivery (CISD)

A **Change in the State of Delivery (CISD)** is an ICT term that denotes a significant alteration in the market's price delivery mechanism. This often signals a shift in market sentiment or a strong rejection from a critical price level, thereby confirming a potential trend reversal. For practical application, traders should look for clear breaks in market structure and decisive price rejections from key levels, such as FVGs or BPRs, particularly on lower timeframes. Such observations can reinforce the higher timeframe bias and provide increased conviction for trade entries.

### 7. Risk Management

Effective **Risk Management** is a cornerstone of the discussed strategy. The trader highlights the importance of a specific "3-4R" (Risk-to-Reward) ratio, aiming for three to four units of reward for every unit of risk taken. Stop-loss orders are strategically placed above recent highs for short positions to protect capital. In practice, traders must define their risk parameters before entering any trade. This involves calculating the stop-loss based on market structure (e.g., placing it above a recent high for a short trade) and ensuring that the potential profit target offers at least a 3:1 or 4:1 risk-to-reward ratio. Adhering to these risk management principles is paramount for achieving long-term profitability in trading.
